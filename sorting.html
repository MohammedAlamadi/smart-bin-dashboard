AI Sorting at HQ

At the headquarters, waste from the city doesn’t just get dumped on a pile. It passes through an AI-powered sorting line that helps rescue recyclables, correct mistakes from the street bins, and measure how “clean” the waste stream really is.

Computer vision
Real-time classification
Corrects wrong bin choices
Vision System
Eyes of the line

A camera is mounted above the conveyor belt. As items move, the system captures images and sends them to an AI model trained to recognise plastics, metals, paper, glass and organic waste.

  • Detects items even when mixed together.
  • Can highlight “wrong bin” items for correction.
  • Builds statistics about what people actually throw.
Conveyor & Actuators
Hands of the line

Once the model decides the material type, the control system triggers small air jets or mechanical arms to push each item into the correct output chute.

  • Separate chutes for plastic, metal, paper, glass, and residuals.
  • Can also route organic waste to the Food → Energy line.
  • Each movement is logged for reporting and analytics.
Connected to Smart Bins
Same data pipeline

The AI sorting line doesn’t work alone. It uses the same backend as the smart bins, so the city can see, for example, how many “Recycle” items actually arrive mixed inside the general waste stream.

  • Helps measure contamination of each district.
  • Supports feedback and education campaigns.
  • Future: adjust collection strategies based on real sorting data.
Sorting Pipeline
Step-by-step

This is how one bag of mixed waste moves through the AI sorting line:

1
Truck unloads waste from the city into the intake hopper at HQ.
2
Items are spread out on the conveyor belt to create a single layer.
3
Camera above the belt captures frames as items pass under it.
4
AI model classifies each item: plastic bottle, aluminium can, paper cup, food waste…
5
Actuators (air jets / arms) push items sideways into the correct chute.
6
System logs item type, confidence and source bin zone for analytics dashboards.

How the AI Decides
Example output

The model returns a material label plus a confidence score. Below is a simplified example of what the system might see for three items on the belt:

Detected Item Predicted Class Confidence Action
Transparent bottle Plastic (PET) 97% Send to recycling stream
Take-away food box Contaminated paper 81% Send to general / residual bin
Apple core Organic food waste 93% Send to Food → Energy line

In the real world, the model would be trained on thousands of labelled images and improved over time as operators correct mistakes. For this university prototype, we focus on explaining the logic and showing how it integrates with the rest of the SmartWaste system.

Why Use AI Sorting?
Benefits

Traditional sorting lines rely heavily on manual labour and simple mechanical separation. Adding AI brings several advantages:

  • Detects materials that are hard to separate mechanically.
  • Improves capture rate of high-value recyclables (metals, certain plastics).
  • Provides continuous data about contamination levels per area or building.
  • Supports adaptive education: if one zone throws a lot of plastic into general waste, the city knows.
Future Extensions
Next steps

In future versions of SmartWaste, the AI sorting line could be extended with:

  • Weight sensors on each chute to calculate exact recovery rates.
  • Automatic quality scoring for each truck load (how “clean” the recyclables are).
  • Integration with dynamic pricing or rewards for buildings that sort well.
  • On-device models at smaller transfer stations, not only at a central HQ.
